# Chapter 2: Literature Survey

A literature survey is a thorough analysis of previous investigations and studies pertaining to a certain issue or topic. It aids in comprehending previous research, spotting holes or restrictions in current systems, and offering information on the technologies, methods, and strategies applied in related initiatives.

### 2.1 Blockchain-based transparency for humanitarian aid distribution
Ziad et al. (2019) presented a comprehensive study on the application of blockchain technology to improve transparency in humanitarian aid distribution and public welfare systems. The authors analyzed the potential of decentralized ledgers to reduce administrative overhead and prevent the misappropriation of resources by providing an immutable audit trail for every transaction. Their findings demonstrated that blockchain-based systems consistently achieve higher levels of public trust and accountability compared to traditional centralized databases, making them highly suitable for large-scale disaster relief operations. By evaluating key parameters such as transaction speed and data integrity, the study highlighted the practical applicability of distributed ledgers in designing corruption-resistant digital ecosystems, particularly for international aid organizations and government welfare bodies.

### 2.2 AI-driven prioritization and resource allocation in disaster triage
Wang et al. (2021) conducted a detailed analysis of artificial intelligence techniques for prioritizing relief requests and optimizing resource allocation during disaster triage. The study evaluated various machine learning models to categorize user-submitted requests based on urgency, category, and geographical location under high-stress operating conditions. Detailed simulations showed that AI-driven systems can significantly reduce the lead time for critical aid delivery while maintaining high accuracy in identifying life-threatening situations. The authors demonstrated that while manual triage is prone to human error and fatigue, intelligent agents provide a more reliable and scalable solution for real-time welfare interventions, making AI-based priority assessment suitable for modern crisis management platforms.

### 2.3 Design and implementation of mobile-based citizen engagement platforms
Sharma et al. (2022) presented the design and implementation of mobile-based citizen engagement platforms for transparent public service delivery, focusing on the performance of real-time request tracking modules. The study analyzed the interaction between beneficiaries and aid providers, evaluating the efficiency of mobile interfaces in collecting qualitative data and providing feedback to users. The authors gathered and implemented a unified mobile framework that allows citizens to submit requests and track their status in real-time, demonstrating significant improvements in user satisfaction and administrative transparency. Their work highlighted the practical potential of mobile-centric welfare designs in reducing the "digital divide" and ensuring that marginalized communities have direct access to essential services.

### 2.4 Comparative analysis of real-time activity logging in public welfare systems
Gupta et al. (2023) conducted a comparative analysis of various real-time activity logging techniques for public welfare systems, focusing on audit trails, activity feeds, and stakeholder notification systems. The study evaluated each logging type in terms of data transparency, accessibility for public auditors, and system performance under heavy load conditions. Detailed calculations of data throughput and query latency highlighted the advantages of role-based logging mechanisms in maintaining a "single source of truth" for all stakeholders. The authors demonstrated that transparent activity logs provide better trade-offs between system security and public accountability, making them suitable for high-integrity humanitarian applications where trust is a primary requirement.

### 2.5 Integrated frameworks for NGO and volunteer coordination
Lee and Roberts (2020) presented a study on the power-efficient design of integrated frameworks for NGO and volunteer coordination, focusing on reducing communication silos in disaster response. The authors designed and implemented a collaborative dashboard that synchronizes volunteer availability with real-time aid requests, optimizing both resource utilization and response times. The study evaluated the performance of the integrated framework in terms of coordination overhead and task completion rates, demonstrating that unified platforms can significantly reduce duplication of efforts compared to fragmented standalone applications. This work highlights the practical applicability of integrated humanitarian tech in streamlining the collaboration between government agencies, non-profits, and spontaneous community volunteers.
